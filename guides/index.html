Lottery Guides & Tips

In-depth, independently researched articles to help you understand Powerball, make informed decisions, and play responsibly.

Why We Wrote These Guides

Powerball is one of the most popular lottery games in the United States, yet reliable, plain-English information about how it actually works is surprisingly hard to find. Most players learn the rules from a hurried explanation at the convenience counter or from scattered forum posts that mix hearsay with outdated numbers. We started writing these guides because we kept getting the same questions from readers of our number generator: What do the odds really mean? How much of the advertised jackpot do winners actually take home? Should I pick my own numbers or use Quick Pick? When is the cut-off time to buy a ticket in my state?

Each article below is written from primary sources — the official Multi-State Lottery Association rules, IRS publications on gambling income, state revenue department guidance, and published academic work on combinatorial probability. We cite specific figures, show the math where it helps, and clearly flag the places where the answer depends on your state or your personal financial situation. Nothing in these guides is affiliate-driven or sponsored. If we mention a third-party service, it is because it is genuinely relevant to the topic, and we never receive payment for the mention.

How to Use This Section

If you have never bought a Powerball ticket before, start with the beginner guide. It walks through the entire experience from picking a retailer through claiming a prize, and it is written with the assumption that you have no prior lottery knowledge. If you already play but want to understand the real mathematics of the game, the odds guide breaks down each of the nine prize tiers with the actual combinatorial calculations rather than the marketing-friendly approximations. And if you are fortunate enough to win a meaningful prize — or simply curious about what would happen if you did — the taxes guide lays out the federal withholding rules, the state-by-state tax landscape, and the lump-sum versus annuity trade-off in concrete dollar terms.

Every guide is a single long-form page so you can read it end to end, bookmark it, or jump to a specific section. We update them when the rules change (for example, when states add online ticket sales or when federal withholding thresholds are adjusted) and note the revision date in the footer of each article.

A Note on Responsible Play

Lottery games are designed as entertainment, not as an investment strategy. The expected value of any Powerball ticket is negative — meaning, on average, every dollar spent returns less than a dollar in prizes over the long run. That is how the game funds its jackpots and its beneficiary programs. Playing occasionally, within a budget you have already decided you can afford to lose, is a reasonable form of entertainment. Playing more than that is not a strategy; it is a warning sign.

If any part of your lottery play is starting to feel compulsive, or if you are spending money you cannot comfortably afford, free and confidential help is available 24 hours a day through the National Problem Gambling Helpline at 1-800-522-4700. You can also visit ncpgambling.org for chat and text options. We mention this on every guide because we think it matters more than any tip we could give about number selection.
